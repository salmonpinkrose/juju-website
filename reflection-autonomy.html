On Autonomy (自主性) in the Age of AI

There is a concept I keep returning to as I build: 自主性 — autonomy. Not in the sense of independence for its own sake, but in the deeper sense of being the initiating force behind your own direction. Of not waiting.

In the context of AI, I think autonomy has become the defining capability — and the one most worth deliberately cultivating.

01The best way to learn AI is to use it, not study it.

AI is moving faster than any curriculum can track. By the time a course is written about a tool, a better tool exists. The only meaningful way to develop genuine fluency is through direct, repeated contact with the technology itself — building things, breaking things, noticing what works and what doesn't. There is no shortcut through observation. The knowledge that matters most in this era is the kind you can only acquire by doing.

02The technical barrier is gone. What remains is judgment.

For most of the history of software, the limiting factor for bringing an idea to life was technical execution. You needed to know how to code, or you needed to find someone who did. That constraint filtered out an enormous amount of potentially valuable thinking before it ever had a chance to become real. That filter is dissolving. With AI-assisted development, the bottleneck has shifted — decisively and permanently — from technical ability to the quality of your thinking. The ability to identify a real problem, understand who it belongs to, imagine a solution that actually fits, and make the right calls when you inevitably have to choose between tradeoffs: these are the skills that matter now. Creativity, curiosity, and judgment have become the primary competitive advantage.

03Autonomy means thinking proactively — finding the gap before anyone names it.

The most valuable thing a product person can do is not execute a clearly defined brief. It is notice something that isn't yet a brief. It is seeing a behaviour that doesn't quite make sense, a workaround that people have normalised, a question that gets asked in every group chat but has no product answer — and recognising that as signal. Autonomy means developing the habit of looking for those gaps without being asked. It means building a kind of peripheral vision for unmet needs, which is ultimately what every meaningful product was built on.

04Autonomy redefines what a single person can own.

In traditional product development, one person could rarely own the full stack of a product — you needed designers, engineers, data analysts, and PMs each covering their slice. Autonomy in the AI era collapses that. A single person with strong judgment and the right tools can now hold the entire product in their head and in their hands — from the insight to the interface to the iteration. That is not a small shift. It fundamentally changes who gets to build, what speed is possible, and how close the person with the idea can stay to the thing being made. The question is no longer "do I have the resources to build this?" It is simply "do I have the judgment to know what's worth building?"

05Autonomy is a compounding skill, not a fixed trait.

The people who will thrive in the AI era are not necessarily the most technically skilled or the most credentialed. They are the ones who keep moving — who try something, learn from it, adjust, and try again without waiting for permission or perfect conditions. Autonomy is not a personality type you either have or don't. It is a practice that compounds. Every project you ship independently makes the next one faster and more confident. Every time you solve a problem with a tool you taught yourself makes the next barrier feel smaller. The gap between people who adapt to AI and people who don't will not be determined by intelligence — it will be determined by who was willing to start before they were ready.
