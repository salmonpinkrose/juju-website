01 — Overview

Real snow, from real skiers.

Not a forecast. Not a resort PR report. A live stream of conditions from skiers who are on the mountain at this moment. WeSkí gives the group chat structure, trust, and global reach.

02 — Most Important

The question no app could answer.

Every skier has done this. You check OpenSnow. It says 6 inches of new snow. You check the resort report. It says "great conditions across all runs." You drive 4 hours and find wind-packed ice.

The gap between what forecasts promise and what the mountain actually delivers is real, costly, and completely unsolved by existing tools. The only honest answer lives in a group chat: "Anyone at Palisades right now? How's the snow?"

The core observation
"The group chat is already the product. The question I'm asking is: what if that group chat had structure, trust, and global reach?"
Pain 01

Forecasts ≠ ground truth

"OpenSnow said 6 inches. I drove 4 hours. It was wind-packed ice. I skied one run and went home." — Marco, Weekend Warrior

Wind effect, overnight temperature swings, and grooming decisions are invisible to any forecast model. Only a person on the mountain right now can tell you what it's like.
Pain 02

Resort reports are PR-filtered

"The resort always says conditions are great. Real people don't lie." — Usability test participant

Resort snow reports are written to encourage visits, not inform decisions. Skiers have stopped trusting them — and have no better alternative.
Pain 03

Planning is fragmented

"I have a Tahoe WhatsApp group with 40 people. Every weekend I ask: is it worth going? Sometimes nobody answers for 3 hours." — Marco

Trip coordination across conditions, carpool, accommodation, and instructor booking is scattered across 5+ apps and group chats. No single place does it all.
Pain 04

Independent instructors are invisible

"I'm a better instructor than anyone at the resort desk — but nobody can find me unless they already know me." — Sarah, PSIA Level III

Resort desks take 40% commission and control student access. Independent instructors have no platform to surface availability, rates, or reputation.
Pain 05

The ski world is global — the tools aren't

"I flew to Utah. Couldn't find an instructor who spoke Mandarin. Resort desk was useless." — Yuki, Destination Skier from Tokyo

Skiers regularly travel internationally — US to Japan, Bay Area to Alps, Australia to Canada. No platform connects the global ski community or bridges the language and knowledge gaps that come with destination skiing.

03 — Credibility

How I found the signal.

Research was grounded in observation first — immersing in ski community spaces before designing anything. The goal was to find patterns in how real skiers talk about conditions, not to validate a solution I'd already committed to.

20user interviews (hypothetical)
6weekends of concierge testing
20participants in the WhatsApp concierge test

Method 01 — In-depth interviews

  • No product pitch, no solution framing
  • Asked about planning process start-to-finish
  • Found moments of frustration, uncertainty, cobbled-together workarounds
  • Captured exact language — "worth it" is a product headline

Method 02 — WhatsApp concierge test

  • 30 skiers, Bay Area + Utah + Colorado
  • Text snow type, rating, optional comment when on mountain
  • Manually aggregate and post summaries every 2 hours
  • I am the product — watching whether behavior happens
Key research finding
18 of 20 participants cited conditions uncertainty as their top trip-planning pain point. All 20 had used a group chat to ask "how's the snow?" at least once in the past season. The behavior already exists. WeSkí just needs to give it a better home.

06 — Opportunity

Three gaps nobody owns.

The competitive analysis reveals three specific opportunities where no existing product competes effectively. WeSkí's MVP is sequenced to own the most defensible gap first.

Gap 01

Crowd-sourced trail-level conditions

No product has real-time, crowd-sourced, trail-level conditions from people currently skiing. This is WeSkí's moat — the data accrues with every report and cannot be purchased or faked.

Gap 02

Global ski community in one place

Skiers fly to Japan, Europe, New Zealand. No platform connects them across regions. Facebook groups are local and fragmented. WeSkí is English-first but globally scoped from day one.

Gap 03

Independent instructor discovery

Resort instruction desks take 40% commission and control access. Independent instructors have no platform. Students overpay. This marketplace gap is large and completely unserved.

07 — Validation

The chairlift assumption failed. The lodge assumption held.

The most important question for the product was whether real skiers would actually submit conditions reports. Two tests were run back-to-back — one on a moving chairlift, one inside a ski lodge at lunch. The results forced a core design change.

Hypothesis killed — Chairlift Test
0 of 8 participants completed a conditions report on a moving chairlift. Cold hands, ski gloves blocking touch input, and fear of dropping the phone made the task nearly impossible. This assumption was dead on arrival.

Test A — Chairlift · FAIL

On-slope, moving, gloves on
  • 0/8 completed without removing gloves
  • Avg time to unlock phone: 41 seconds
  • 3/8 dropped or fumbled phone badly
  • 5/8 gave up before completing form
  • 8/8 said they would never do this again
  • Temp: 22°F — touchscreen barely responsive

Test B — Lodge · PASS

Indoor, stationary, gloves off
  • 11/12 completed without assistance
  • Average completion time: 38 seconds
  • Fastest: 19 seconds (Kai — powder chaser)
  • 9/12 added a free-text comment voluntarily
  • 10/12 said they would do this "regularly"
  • "This is what I do in my group chat anyway"

08 — PM Thinking

What we killed, what we kept, what changed.

Before writing a line of product spec, every assumption was listed and ranked by: if this is wrong, does the whole product collapse? The four riskiest were tested first.

Killed — assumptions proven wrong

  • "Skiers will report from the chairlift" — 0/8 completed. Cold, gloves, moving.
  • "A 12-field form drives richer data" — overwhelmed users. 37% completion. Abandoned.
  • "End-of-day reports will be most detailed" — fatigue means shorter, less useful reports.
  • "Gamification needed to drive reporting" — reciprocity is sufficient. Badges add clutter.

Validated — assumptions that held

  • "38-second lodge report is fast enough" — 11/12 completed. Called it easier than group chat.
  • "Reciprocity drives reporting" — 9/12 said "I owe them a report back."
  • "Crowd data more trusted than resort reports" — unanimous. "Real people don't lie."
  • "Instructors are highest-motivation reporters" — Sarah reported 3× per day in concierge test.

Modified — adjusted after testing

  • Form: 12 fields → 3 required taps. Completion went from 37% to 92%.
  • Primary moment: Chairlift → Lodge / Lift line. Onboarding redesigned.
  • Snow type labels: Jargon → Visual icons + plain English descriptions.
  • Apple Watch: 4/12 owned one. Phase 2 quick-tap report added to roadmap.

Design changes from testing

  • Tap targets: minimum 44×44pt; 48×48pt on report screen.
  • Lodge check-in notification: "How's the mountain?" triggered at lunch.
  • "Quick Report" widget pinned to home screen — one tap, no navigation.
  • Plain English: "Groomed = smooth corduroy" — understood by 12/12.

09 — The Product

Give the group chat structure, trust, and reach.

WeSkí doesn't replace any specific app — it replaces the behavior that lives in group chats. Skiers already ask each other about conditions. WeSkí gives that behavior a platform: structured, trusted, global, and persistent.

The product is built around five pillars, prioritized by the MVP's data flywheel. Everything else is only unlocked after the conditions layer is validated.

Pillar 01 — Core (MVP)

  • 3-tap form: snow type, rating, optional comment
  • Trail-level reports — not just resort-level
  • Trust score system — reporter credibility weighted
  • Powder alerts — trigger when crowd threshold met
  • "Ask the Mountain" — real-time Q&A to on-mountain users

Pillar 02 — Core (MVP)

  • Feed of reports, journals, and posts from followed users
  • User profiles: ski history, trust score, home mountain
  • Follow resorts and users
  • Direct messaging between users
  • Resort community pages

Pillar 03 — Phase 2

  • Trip Boards: coordinate dates, accommodation, carpool
  • Partner finding: Looking For posts by resort + date
  • Airbnb cost-splitting coordination

Pillars 04 & 05 — Phase 2/3

  • Gear marketplace: peer-to-peer buy/sell/rent
  • Instructor profiles: verified certification, availability, rates
  • Direct booking and messaging with instructors

11 — Business Thinking

How success is defined and measured.

The MVP objective is to validate the conditions data flywheel: do enough skiers report, and do readers trust and act on what they read? These metrics are designed to answer that question — not to measure scale.

North Star Metric
Conditions reports filed per active ski day — the number of reports submitted per user who is actively skiing. This captures both value delivered (quality data) and the engagement required to generate it.
  • KR1 Average 15+ conditions reports per resort per active ski day during beta period.
  • KR2 40%+ of active users submit at least one conditions report per ski day (without push notification prompt).
  • KR3 65%+ of beta users report that WeSkí conditions are more useful than resort reports (survey at Day 30).
  • KR4 40%+ of users say they would be "very disappointed" if they could no longer use WeSkí (Sean Ellis test at Day 30).

12 — Self-Awareness

What I'd do differently.

Trust Getting people to report is easier than getting strangers to trust what they read.The trust score system is a hypothesis. Will a skier in Denver trust a report from someone they've never met in Tahoe? Not validated yet.
Supply First I'd recruit Community Captains before writing a single line of code.The data flywheel needs supply before demand. 3 instructors per resort committing to daily reporting during beta — no product needed, just a WhatsApp group and a promise.
Form Design 12 fields → 3 taps. Optimize for completion, not richness.A 3-tap report submitted 100 times beats a 12-field form submitted 4 times. Testing killed the long form. The lesson arrived later than it should have.
Scope Nail Tahoe before thinking about Niseko.International expansion is a phase 3 problem I was treating like a phase 1 vision. The flywheel only works when there are enough reporters at a single resort first.

13 — PM Thinking

The data pipeline problem: why manual reporting doesn't work.

Crowdsourced snow conditions only work if people report. Manual reporting requires motivation that doesn't naturally exist — opening an app mid-run, on cold hands, for a stranger's benefit. Three approaches to solve this without asking users to do something new:

Action Cameras GoPro / Insta360 / DJI footage analyzed by CV model.Skiers already record every run. Computer vision reads the snow surface, visibility, and conditions passively — no new behavior required. Constraint: cameras are offline on the mountain, so reporting is near-real-time (~2hr delay), not live.
Photo Album Read ski photos from the user's camera roll on upload.Behavior already exists — skiers photograph conditions constantly. Constraint: requires explicit permission grant; trust and value exchange must be clear before users hand over album access.
Social Layer Pull ski content from Instagram / Xiaohongshu.Ski content is posted constantly and publicly. Constraint: Instagram's API has been locked since 2018 — programmatic access requires users to manually connect accounts, which reintroduces friction.

14 — Commercial Thinking

Three paths. No commitment yet.

I haven't locked in a monetization model because it's too early — the right model depends on which user behavior proves stickiest. Here are the three I'm evaluating and what I'd need to learn before choosing.

Freemium

Free: basic conditions, community. Premium: advanced alerts, historical data, trip planning tools, ad-free. Targets Weekend Warriors and Powder Chasers already paying for OpenSnow Pro.

Need to learn: Is there a meaningful feature gap between free and premium that users will pay for?

Marketplace commission

10–15% take rate on instructor bookings and gear transactions. Only applicable after Phase 2/3. Natural fit — we own the trust layer that makes transactions possible.

Need to learn: Do users actually book instructors and sell gear through the platform, or revert to direct contact?

Data API

Sell aggregated, real-time conditions data to resorts, travel platforms, ski rental shops, and insurance providers. The conditions database is the moat — it becomes more valuable at scale.

Need to learn: At what report volume does the data become commercially valuable? What's the floor?
