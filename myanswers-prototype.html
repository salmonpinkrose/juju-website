The Method6

Why conversation works

Traditional tests ask you to choose between someone else's words. Agree or disagree. The result is a label applied to a simplified version of you.

But language carries personality. The words you reach for, the stories you tell, the things you choose to mention — all of these are data your personality has been generating your whole life.

The conversation in this book draws on Person-Centred and Narrative Therapy — following your words, not redirecting them. The AI behaves like a therapist: curious, non-directive, always following your thread.

The most honest answers are the ones you didn't know you had.

How they think
INFJs live inside a rich internal world of patterns and meaning. They see connections others miss and tend to understand how things will unfold before they do.
How they feel
They absorb the emotions of people around them like a sponge. Deeply caring but intensely private — they give a lot and protect their inner world fiercely.

A need for meaning above all else. INFJs can endure almost anything if they understand why. Drawn to work that serves something larger than themselves.

Chapter Two

On Relationships

Session Two · April 2025

She has always loved people the way she loves a good book — slowly, carefully, rereading the same passages until she understands what they were really trying to say.

The people she keeps close are few. This is not because she is cold, or because she finds others uninteresting. It is because she takes relationships seriously, the way a person takes a vow seriously — and she has learned, through the particular education of being misread, that not everyone is prepared for the depth she arrives with.

She spoke, in this session, about a friendship that had quietly ended. Not dramatically. No argument, no rupture — just the slow realisation that the other person had been seeing someone else when they looked at her. A version. A surface. She had outgrown the reflection.

"I don't think she knew me," she said. "I think she knew who she needed me to be."

There is a particular loneliness in being misunderstood by someone who loves you.

Your Autobiography18

What she is learning — slowly, with the resistance of someone who has always been more comfortable giving than receiving — is that being truly known requires a different kind of courage than she expected.

Not the courage to be vulnerable. She has that. She has always had that, in abundance, often to her own detriment. What she is learning is the courage to be patient with the process of being known. To stay in the room while someone else figures out who she is, even when their version is still incomplete.

She is, at her core, someone who believes in people's capacity to surprise her. This is both her greatest strength and, occasionally, her most expensive habit.

"I think I'm finally learning the difference between intimacy and familiarity. They look the same from the outside. But one of them requires you to keep showing up even after the other person has stopped surprising you."

The relationships she is building now are different. Quieter. Less urgent. She is no longer trying to be understood all at once.

She is learning to let herself be discovered gradually — the way light enters a room at dawn, not all at once but incrementally, until suddenly everything is visible.

The next chapter will ask: who are the people she is building this with?
